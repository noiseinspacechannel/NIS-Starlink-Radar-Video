What Is ICI Leakage?

Each OFDM subcarrier is a sinc(f) function in frequency. Normally, the receiver's FFT bins are positioned exactly at each sinc peak — where every neighboring sinc has a null. This is orthogonality.

  • Doppler shifts ALL subcarriers uniformly. They do not move relative to each other.
  • Because the receiver's FFT grid is fixed, after a shift, every bin samples its subcarrier off-peak and picks up non-zero leakage from neighbors.
  • The colored dots show the power recovered from the intended subcarrier. The white dots show total power in that bin (including leakage).

How Does SAR Geometry Create Residual ICI?

Even if you track the beam center's Doppler perfectly, the compensation fails at the beam edges. Why? Because the squint angle varies across the azimuth swath. A target at the leading edge of the beam has a higher closing velocity than a target at the beam center. This creates a residual Doppler spread across the beam. Wider swaths → more spread → more ICI.

Compensation Strategies

  • Rx LO Tracking: The receiver shifts its mixing frequency to match the beam center's Doppler shift. Tx stays at perfect baseband. Beam edges see residual shift.
  • Tx Pre-compensation: The transmitter pre-shifts its waveform in the opposite direction so that after propagation, the beam center lands perfectly on the Rx baseband grid. (See Tx plot move!)
  • Wider Subcarriers: The simplest ICI mitigation. If you make subcarrier BW (Δf) much larger than the edge Doppler spread, the residual shift becomes a tiny fraction of Δf, restoring orthogonality.

Why is it worse at higher Carrier Frequencies?

Doppler shift is proportional to the carrier frequency: f_d = (v / c) * f_c. As you move from Ku-band (12 GHz) to Ka-band (30 GHz) or V-band (50 GHz), the absolute Doppler spread across the beam increases linearly. If you keep the subcarrier bandwidth (Δf) constant, the spread becomes a much larger percentage of Δf, leading to severe ICI. You must use wider subcarriers at higher carrier frequencies to compensate!

What about Total Bandwidth?

Doppler shift scales with the exact frequency of each subcarrier. A subcarrier at f_c - BW/2 will have a slightly smaller absolute Doppler shift than a subcarrier at f_c + BW/2. When the Total BW is very wide (e.g., several GHz), the Doppler shift varies significantly across the OFDM symbol itself. Even if you center-track the middle subcarrier perfectly, the subcarriers at the far edges of the band will suffer from their own residual shift, causing Subcarrier Spread ICI. This degradation is quantified as "Edge Loss" under the Subcarrier Spread metric, showing the worst-case SNR penalty at the extreme edges of the frequency band and the outer edges of the azimuth swath (where the squint angle, and thus Doppler scaling, is most severe).

What About HRWS Mode?

HRWS (High Resolution Wide Swath) typically uses multiple parallel receive channels distributed in azimuth, not elevation. Because the phase centers are separate along the velocity vector, each channel observes a slightly different squint angle to the same target at any given instant. While HRWS reconstructs a wider unambiguous Doppler spectrum, the instantaneous OFDM ICI problem remains similar for each channel individually, driven by the broad transmit beam.
